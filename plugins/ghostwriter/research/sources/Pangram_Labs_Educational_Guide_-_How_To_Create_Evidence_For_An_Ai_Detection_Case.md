# How to collect evidence for an AI academic integrity case

**By Bradley Emi**  
**March 13, 2025**

> *We would like to thank Marilyn Derby, Associate Director, Student Support and Judicial Affairs at UC Davis, for her contribution to the ideas in this piece and for some of the visual resources used in this post. We would also like to thank Amanda Clarke, English department chair at Viewpoint School in California, for her excellent reference on distinguishing the characteristics of student writing versus AI writing.*

## Introduction

Perhaps you've received an essay and you have a gut feeling that the writing does not belong to the student in your class. You run it through Pangram, and it comes back 99.9% confident that the writing is AI-generated.

Or perhaps you are an academic integrity officer, and a professor reports a student's work for AI-generated plagiarism, yet, the student and the parent absolutely insist that the student wrote it themself.

You read the writing and it has all of the telltale signs of AI writing. "In today's technological era," the writing begins. The student explains that an author "weaves the details intricately together through a rich tapestry of perspectives." The grammatically perfect, evenly structured essay ends with the classic phrase "In conclusion,..." or maybe "Overall,..."

You know deep down that your student has not written the assignment, but you just can't prove it. When the people you're trying to convince say, "AI detectors don't work and can't be trusted," or "It is impossible to know for sure," what do you do?

A positive AI detection is just the beginning of the conversation, and can never stand alone when punitive action against a student is being considered. A holistic approach should be used when the stakes are high, and more evidence needs to be collected after the AI detection score in order to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that a student's work is inauthentic or unoriginal.

Below are seven strategies to collect additional evidence for such cases.

## 1. Accumulate more detailed textual evidence

AI-generated writing is never "given away" by one particular phrase or word choice. AI detection algorithms make their decisions based on accumulation of many weak signals in the text. Similarly, you can look for many of the signals present in AI-generated text, and use them all in totality to demonstrate that the AI signals could not have shown up by random chance.

Start by looking for common AI phrases and see if they show up often. In clear-cut cases, the AI-generated writing contains so many of these that it is very hard to argue that they are a coincidence.

You can reference a [comprehensive list of commonly used AI vocabulary words and phrasing patterns](https://github.com/jenna-russell/human_detectors/blob/main/prompts/detection_guide.txt) as a resource. AI detection tools like Pangram can pull out these phrases automatically along with their frequencies. It's important to understand that no one of these phrases is proof that the text was AI-generated, but many of these phrases in combination is very strong evidence because it becomes vanishingly unlikely that all of these phrases would have appeared by coincidence.

Beyond the individual words and phrases level, you can also look for high-level characteristics of AI writing:

- **Vague generalities**: AI writing does not like to get into specific evidence, preferring to speak in vague generalities. If it does provide details, it may hallucinate those details. Direct student reflections upon the work and specific textual citations are characteristics of student writing that AI chatbots cannot emulate yet.
- **Lack of genuine reflection**: AI writing will not genuinely reflect on a topic from the perspective of a student.
- **Perfect grammar and neutral voice**: AI writing uses consistently perfect grammar and a smooth, neutral voice.

It is also worth noting that when a student's authentic writing is mixed with AI-generated writing, there can often be abrupt shifts in tone and style.

## 2. Collect proof of process

When work is written by a student, it happens as a result of a process: brainstorming, outlining, drafting, revising, and proofreading. When work is plagiarized from generative AI, it is often just copied and pasted.

A simple way to check in on a student's proof of writing process is to ask them for artifacts: ask them for their notes, brainstorms, and outlines. If it is a final draft, ask to see their rough draft. Many times this is enough to see proof of writing process—innocent students are unafraid to prove it, and cheating students often simply cannot produce these artifacts.

Several tools are available to check a student's writing process:

- [Draftback](https://draftback.com/) - A Chrome extension that replays the student's writing history in Google Docs
- [Brisk Teaching](https://www.briskteaching.com/ai-tools-for-teachers)
- [Cursive Technologies](https://cursivetechnology.com/)
- [Visible AI](https://www.visibleai.io/)

When used in combination with AI detection tools, these can be powerful resources. For example, Draftback traces can show where a student was editing their writing, or if there was one big copy-and-paste action.

**Important caveat**: Writing process tools alone should not be seen as bulletproof evidence. Knowing that teachers check revision history, students are aware that a copy and paste leaves them vulnerable. Some students will transcribe ChatGPT outputs into their document to make it appear they wrote it themselves. Additionally, there now exist software tools that fake revision history, such as "Human Auto Typer" Chrome extensions. While examining writing process and revision history can be useful, there are now ways that students can circumvent these simple checks.

## 3. Check for citation validity

Generative AI will often make up citations, misquote sources, and make other mistakes in attributing work that are obvious to spot. When AI chatbots do not know what sources back up a claim they make, most of the time they are happy to simply make up fictitious citations.

Citation mistakes are often some of the most compelling evidence in AI cases because an intentional falsification of a research source is in itself an academic integrity violation. You can often simply look at the bibliography or the works cited and check to see if the entries represent real papers. If you Google the first paper and it is not a real paper, that is incredibly strong evidence of a violation.

**Important caveat**: Real citations do not indicate for sure that the student did not use AI. New tools such as Deep Research and Perplexity actually do cite correct sources, and chatbots are rapidly improving at not hallucinating false sources.

## 4. Validate that the student understands their own writing

One of the easiest ways to check if a student's work is original versus falsified is simply by asking them questions about the paper. If the writing level of the submission does not match the writing level of the student, ask them about the most complex parts of the writing.

For younger students, asking them about the meaning of a complicated word that ChatGPT uses often that students at that level never use (such as "axiomatic") can be enough to get the student to admit they used AI.

At the university level, where students may be expected to come up with novel, original ideas, you may want to ask them questions about how they came up with the idea. This can lead into a discussion about writing process where you can collect information about how the writing came together.

**Approach with empathy**: An academic integrity discussion with students can be highly stressful, and the student may be defensive when presented with evidence. Frame the conversation to come to a meaningful understanding of what happened, so that you can help the student succeed in the future. Give the student a chance to correct their mistakes and explain why they needed to resort to using AI. Be open to the fact that the use of AI may have been the result of a misunderstanding rather than an intentional act of wrongdoing.

## 5. Compare the work to the student's previous samples

Particularly applicable to younger or developing students, AI writing is often significantly ahead of the writing level that one might expect from a student's previous work.

Recommend pulling up instances of previous writing from a student. Universities often have central databases where essays from other classes can be pulled. If the student is new to you, ask their previous teacher for a few writing samples from that student.

A sudden jump in writing level from a student who writes poorly to a student who writes with perfect spelling and grammar is cause to be concerned.

## 6. Compare the work to the ChatGPT response

ChatGPT does not often have a lot of variance in its output. When you paste the same prompt into ChatGPT twice, it will not return the exact same text, but often it will have many similarities that are difficult to be made by coincidence.

You can generate the ChatGPT response using the assignment as a prompt if you know it. Then compare the submission side-by-side with the ChatGPT output, looking for similar phrases, structure, and semantic meaning.

Another tactic is to generate multiple responses from ChatGPT and look at the similarity. If the submission cannot be picked out of the bunch easily, then it is likely AI as well.

If the assignment is unknown, you can still come up with a reasonable prompt. Try to figure out a prompt that is specific enough that it would produce an essay like the one you are looking at. Using ChatGPT itself can be a useful resource for this: paste the essay into ChatGPT and ask what the main ideas, topics, and questions the paper is addressing, then try multiple prompts to see what produces reasonably similar essays semantically.

## 7. Gather more opinions

According to research from the University of Maryland, experts can be 92.7% accurate at determining whether or not a text is AI-generated. However, a council of 5 experts, when looking at the majority vote, can be nearly perfect—in the 300 texts that the researchers studied, the majority was perfectly 100% accurate.

Train others in your department or at school on how to detect AI-generated text by eye, so that you can get multiple opinions when there are difficult cases. Talking through some of the different signals that each individual judge picks up on is a great way to gain more confidence in assessing the authenticity of a piece of writing.

Additionally, using a panel of multiple people in determining whether or not a student has violated academic integrity can not only help you be more accurate, but it should help your process be more fair as well. Individuals can be subconsciously or consciously biased in their decision-making for reasons outside the control of the student.

## The importance of additional evidence in AI cases

No single piece of evidence is absolutely foolproof in determining the outcome of the case, but the more evidence that you can collect and accumulate, the more fair and defensible your academic integrity process can be. A holistic approach combining multiple lines of evidence provides the strongest foundation for investigating suspected AI-generated student work.
