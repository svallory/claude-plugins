# Contrasting Linguistic Patterns in Human and LLM-Generated News Text

Alberto Muñoz-Ortiz, Carlos Gómez-Rodríguez, David Vilares

Universidade da Coruña, CITIC, Departamento de Ciencias de la Computación y Tecnologías de la Información, A Coruña, Spain

**DOI:** [10.1007/s10462-024-10903-2](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10462-024-10903-2)

## Abstract

We conduct a quantitative analysis contrasting human-written English news text with comparable large language model (LLM) output from six different LLMs covering three different families and four sizes in total. Our analysis spans several measurable linguistic dimensions, including morphological, syntactic, psychometric, and sociolinguistic aspects. The results reveal various measurable differences between human and AI-generated texts:

- Human texts exhibit more scattered sentence length distributions, more variety of vocabulary, a distinct use of dependency and constituent types, shorter constituents, and more optimized dependency distances
- Humans tend to exhibit stronger negative emotions (such as fear and disgust) and less joy compared to LLM text
- LLM toxicity increases as model size grows
- LLM outputs use more numbers, symbols and auxiliaries (suggesting objective language) and more pronouns than human texts
- The sexist bias prevalent in human text is expressed by LLMs and magnified in all but one model
- Differences between LLMs and humans are larger than between LLMs

**Keywords:** Large language models, Computational linguistics, Machine-generated text, Linguistic biases

## Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) output fluent, human-like text in many languages, with English being the best represented. A crucial question is whether their storytelling strategies align with the linguistic patterns observed in human-generated texts. Do these models use more flowery or redundant vocabulary? Do they exhibit preferences for specific voices or syntactic structures? Are they prone to certain psychometric dimensions?

To contrast linguistic patterns between humans and machines in a controlled environment, we study six generative large language models: Mistral 7B, Falcon 7B, and four models (7B, 13B, 30B and 65B) from the LLaMa family. We contrast several linguistic patterns against human text using English news text, recovering human-generated news and asking the models to generate a news paragraph based on the headline and first words of the news.

We analyze various linguistic patterns including:
- Vocabulary distribution and richness
- Sentence length
- Part-of-speech tags
- Syntactic structures
- Psychometric features (tone and emotions)
- Sociolinguistic aspects (gender bias)

Data and scripts are available at: https://zenodo.org/records/11186264

## Data Preparation

### Data Collection

We generate the evaluation dataset using news published after the release date of the models to ensure they did not have exposure to this content during pre-training. We use New York Times news accessed through its Archive API, gathering all articles available between October 1, 2023, and January 24, 2024, resulting in a dataset of 13,371 articles.

The collected articles primarily consist of news pieces, though around 26% also include other types of texts such as reviews, editorials or obituaries. We focus on two fields: the headline and the lead paragraph.

### Methodological Rationale

We focused our study on English to balance the depth of analysis with practical constraints. The LLMs used are English-centric: LLaMa's dataset comprises over 70% English content, and Falcon's over 80%. This choice allows us to establish a baseline for future studies incorporating multilingual analysis.

We used non-instruction-tuned models to evaluate the effects of pretraining processes and model size on linguistic patterns without the additional layer of instruction-tuning training.

### Generation Process

For text generation, we initialize the models with the headline and the first three words of the human-generated lead paragraph to help the model start and follow the topic. We generate text with the following hyperparameters: temperature of 0.7, top p of 0.9, and a repetition penalty of 1.1. Generated content is limited to 200 tokens to match typical lead paragraph lengths.

### Selected Models

**LLaMa models (Touvron et al. 2023):** Four models (7B, 13B, 30B, 65B) trained on diverse data sources, predominantly in English. Based on the Transformer architecture with innovations from other large language models.

**Falcon 7B:** Trained on 1.5 trillion tokens from curated and web datasets. Features multigroup attention, Rotary Embeddings, standard GeLU activation, and parallel attention/MLP blocks.

**Mistral 7B:** Surpasses larger LLaMa models in various benchmarks. Features Sliding Window Attention, Rolling Buffer Cache, and Prefill and Chunking. Training data not publicly disclosed.

## Analysis of Linguistic Patterns

### Morphosyntactic Analysis

#### Sentence Length

The six LLMs exhibit similar distributions across sentence lengths with less variation compared to human-generated sentences. Models generate sentences more frequently in the 10-30 token range, whereas humans produce longer sentences with greater frequency. This may be explained by the stochastic nature of models streamlining outputs and reducing extreme events. Additionally, humans apply specific writing styles based on genre, while LLMs exhibit "register leveling"—a phenomenon where distinct linguistic features become less pronounced, resulting in more homogenized output.

#### Vocabulary Richness

Using standardized type-token ratio (STTR) and Measure of Textual Lexical Diversity (MTLD), humans demonstrate richer vocabulary than LLMs. LLaMa models come closest to human texts, followed by Mistral, with Falcon exhibiting the lowest lexical diversity. Language model family is more important than model size when accounting for vocabulary richness, as training data is largely shared between models of the same family.

#### Part-of-Speech Tag Distributions

LLMs and humans show consistent differences in grammatical category usage:
- Humans use more nouns and adjectives (content words)
- Humans use punctuation more frequently
- LLMs rely more on symbols and numbers (suggesting objective language)
- LLMs use pronouns and auxiliaries more frequently
- Larger models show greater similarity to humans, though differences between differently-sized models are much smaller than between models and humans

#### Dependencies

**Dependency Arc Lengths:** All LLMs except Falcon show nearly identical dependency lengths. Falcon uses longer dependencies, more similar to human texts. However, Falcon uses more leftward dependencies than both humans and other LLMs.

**Optimality of Dependencies:** While humans produce longer dependencies in absolute terms, their word order is more optimized to minimize dependency lengths. Human texts show noticeably larger Ω (omega) optimality scores, indicating greater optimization. All LLMs show similar Ω distributions, with humans being the outlier.

#### Constituent Analysis

**Constituent Lengths:** While human sentences are longer overall, the average length of sentence constituents for LLMs is greater than for humans. Falcon 7B displays the largest differences among language models.

**Constituent Types:** Human-generated content shows heightened use of noun phrases (NP), adjectival phrases (ADJP), and prepositional phrases (PP). LLMs exhibit more pronounced propensity for verb phrases (VP) and use substantially more subordinate clauses (SBAR). Despite similar frequency of verb UPOS tags, LLMs generate more verb phrases, consistent with increased auxiliary verb usage.

### Semantic Analysis

#### Emotions

Using the Hartmann emotion model, we analyzed emotions in texts. A substantial majority of lead paragraphs are classified as neutral (over 50% across all models and humans).

Key findings:
- Human texts demonstrate greater inclination toward negative emotions like disgust and fear
- LLMs generate more texts with positive emotions, particularly joy and surprise
- Humans and LLMs generate roughly the same amount of angry texts
- Fear increases with LLaMa model size, making larger models more akin to human texts
- Mistral comes closest to expressing emotions similar to humans
- Model toxicity increases as they scale up in size

#### Text Similarity

Using the all-mpnet-base-v2 sentence similarity model, we analyzed cosine semantic similarity between LLM-generated and human-authored lead paragraphs. Smaller-sized LLMs do not necessarily result in decreased sentence similarity compared to human text. Differences across model families are negligible.

#### Gender Bias

Based on pronoun morphology, we analyzed masculine and feminine pronoun usage:
- Human texts use male pronouns 1.71 times more frequently than female pronouns
- All LLM models but Falcon 7B exacerbate this bias (LLaMa models by ~15-17%)
- Falcon 7B reduces the bias by 7.5%
- Mistral 7B increases the male-female ratio by 3%

This disparity may be related to LLMs writing more generic pronouns than humans, exacerbating the disparity between male and female references as they appear in news.

## Conclusion

Despite generating highly fluent text, LLMs exhibit noticeable differences compared to human-generated news texts:

**Lexical Level:** LLMs rely on more restricted vocabulary, with model family being more important than size.

**Morphosyntactic Level:** LLMs prefer parts of speech suggesting objectivity (symbols, numbers) while using fewer adjectives. They use more auxiliary verbs and verb phrases but fewer noun and prepositional phrases. All tested LLMs choose word orders that optimize dependency lengths less than humans.

**Semantic Level:** While showing great text similarity to human texts, models manifest less propensity for aggressive negative emotions like fear or anger. Larger models show increased toxicity.

**Bias:** All models except Falcon 7B exacerbate the male pronoun bias present in human text.

### Future Directions

Future work should explore:
- Analysis across multiple languages
- Comparison of instruction-based models
- Extension to multiple domains beyond news
- Methodological improvements for more comprehensive analysis
