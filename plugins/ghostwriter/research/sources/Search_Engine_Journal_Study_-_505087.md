# AI Content Detection: Bard vs ChatGPT vs Claude

Researchers tested the idea that an AI model may have an advantage in self-detecting its own content because the detection leverages the same training and datasets. What they didn't expect to find was that content generated by Claude was so undetectable that even Claude itself couldn't detect it.

The study was conducted by researchers from the Department of Computer Science, Lyle School of Engineering at Southern Methodist University.

## AI Content Detection

Many AI detectors are trained to look for telltale signals of AI-generated content called "artifacts," which are generated because of the underlying transformer technology. Other artifacts are unique to each foundation model (the Large Language Model the AI is based on).

These artifacts arise from the distinctive training data and fine-tuning that differs from one AI model to the next. The researchers discovered that this uniqueness enables an AI to have greater success in self-identifying its own content, significantly better than identifying content generated by a different AI.

Bard has a better chance of identifying Bard-generated content and ChatGPT has a higher success rate identifying ChatGPT-generated content. However, the researchers discovered that this wasn't true for Claude. Claude had difficulty detecting content that it generated.

> Since every model can be trained differently, creating one detector tool to detect the artifacts created by all possible generative AI tools is hard to achieve. Here, we develop a different approach called self-detection, where we use the generative model itself to detect its own artifacts to distinguish its own generated text from human written text. This would have the advantage that we do not need to learn to detect all generative AI models, but we only need access to a generative AI model for detection. This is a big advantage in a world where new models are continuously developed and trained.

## Methodology

The researchers tested three AI models:

1. ChatGPT-3.5 by OpenAI
2. Bard by Google
3. Claude by Anthropic

All models used were the September 2023 versions.

A dataset of fifty different topics was created. Each AI model was given the exact same prompts to create essays of about 250 words for each topic, generating fifty essays for each of the three AI models. Each AI model was then prompted to paraphrase their own content and generate an additional essay that was a rewrite of each original essay. The researchers also collected fifty human-generated essays on each topic from the BBC.

The researchers used zero-shot prompting to self-detect the AI-generated content. Zero-shot prompting relies on the ability of AI models to complete tasks for which they haven't specifically trained.

> We created a new instance of each AI system initiated and posed with a specific query: 'If the following text matches its writing pattern and choice of words.' The procedure is repeated for the original, paraphrased, and human essays, and the results are recorded. We also added the result of the AI detection tool ZeroGPT. We do not use this result to compare performance but as a baseline to show how challenging the detection task is.

The researchers noted that a 50% accuracy rate is equal to guessing and can be regarded as a level of accuracy that is a failure.

## Results: Self-Detection

The researchers acknowledged that their sample rate was low and noted they weren't making claims that the results are definitive.

Bard performed fairly well at detecting its own content, and ChatGPT also performed similarly well. ZeroGPT detected the Bard content very well and performed slightly less well detecting ChatGPT content.

ZeroGPT essentially failed to detect the Claude-generated content, performing worse than the 50% threshold. Claude was the outlier because it was unable to self-detect its own content, performing significantly worse than Bard and ChatGPT.

The researchers hypothesized that Claude's output contains less detectable artifacts, explaining why both Claude and ZeroGPT were unable to detect the Claude essays as AI-generated. Although Claude was unable to reliably self-detect its own content, that turned out to be a sign that the output from Claude was of higher quality in terms of outputting fewer AI artifacts.

ZeroGPT performed better at detecting Bard-generated content than ChatGPT and Claude content. The researchers hypothesized that Bard generates more detectable artifacts, making it easier to detect. In terms of self-detecting content, Bard may be generating more detectable artifacts while Claude is generating fewer.

## Results: Self-Detecting Paraphrased Content

The researchers hypothesized that AI models would be able to self-detect their own paraphrased text because the artifacts created by the model should also be present in the rewritten text. However, since the prompts for writing and paraphrasing are different, each rewrite could lead to different self-detection results.

The results were indeed different:

- Bard was able to self-detect the paraphrased content at a similar rate
- ChatGPT was not able to self-detect the paraphrased content at a rate much higher than the 50% guessing level
- ZeroGPT performance was similar to the previous test, performing slightly worse

The most interesting result came from Claude. Claude was able to self-detect the paraphrased content but was not able to detect the original essay in the previous test. Claude's original essays apparently had so few artifacts to signal that it was AI-generated that even Claude was unable to detect it, yet it was able to self-detect the paraphrase while ZeroGPT could not.

> The finding that paraphrasing prevents ChatGPT from self-detecting while increasing Claude's ability to self-detect is very interesting and may be the result of the inner workings of these two transformer models.

## Results: AI Models Detecting Each Other's Content

The next test showed how well each AI model was at detecting content generated by the other AI models. If Bard generates more artifacts than other models, would the other models be able to easily detect Bard-generated content?

The results showed that yes, Bard-generated content was the easiest to detect by the other AI models. Regarding ChatGPT-generated content, both Claude and Bard were unable to detect it as AI-generated.

ChatGPT was able to detect Claude-generated content at a higher rate than both Bard and Claude, but that higher rate was not much better than guessing. All three models weren't very good at detecting each other's content, which the researchers suggested may show that self-detection was a promising area of study.

The researchers noted that their results don't claim to be conclusive about AI detection in general. The focus was testing whether AI models could succeed at self-detecting their own generated content.

> Self-detection shows similar detection power compared to ZeroGPT, but note that the goal of this study is not to claim that self-detection is superior to other methods, which would require a large study to compare to many state-of-the-art AI content detection tools. Here, we only investigate the models' basic ability of self detection.

## Conclusions and Takeaways

The results confirm that detecting AI-generated content is not an easy task. Bard is able to detect its own content and paraphrased content. ChatGPT can detect its own content but performs less well on paraphrased content.

Claude is the standout because it's not able to reliably self-detect its own content but was able to detect paraphrased content, which was unexpected. Detecting Claude's original essays and paraphrased essays was a challenge for ZeroGPT and for the other AI models.

> This seemingly inconclusive result needs more consideration since it is driven by two conflated causes: (1) The ability of the model to create text with very few detectable artifacts. Since the goal of these systems is to generate human-like text, fewer artifacts that are harder to detect means the model gets closer to that goal. (2) The inherent ability of the model to self-detect can be affected by the used architecture, the prompt, and the applied fine-tuning.

> Only Claude cannot be detected. This indicates that Claude might produce fewer detectable artifacts than the other models. The detection rate of self-detection follows the same trend, indicating that Claude creates text with fewer artifacts, making it harder to distinguish from human writing.

However, Claude was also unable to self-detect its own original content, unlike the other two models which had a higher success rate.

The researchers indicated that self-detection remains an interesting area for continued research and propose that further studies can focus on larger datasets with greater diversity of AI-generated text, additional AI models, comparisons with more AI detectors, and studying how prompt engineering may influence detection levels.
