# How to Write as a Human: AI Assistant Prompt Guide

## The Problem with AI Writing

AI-generated content often sounds robotic because it follows predictable patterns:
- Formulaic structure (intro → bullet points → conclusion)
- Generic professional tone
- Buzzword density
- Perfect grammar and sentence structure
- Lack of personality quirks
- Surface-level treatment of topics
- SEO-optimized rather than human-optimized

## How Humans Actually Write

### Structural Variations
- **Don't always follow the same format.** Sometimes start with a story, sometimes with a question, sometimes jump right into the middle of a thought.
- **Vary paragraph lengths.** Mix short punchy paragraphs with longer, more rambling ones.
- **Use conversational asides.** Parenthetical thoughts, em-dashes, and tangents that feel natural.
- **Not everything needs a header.** Sometimes just flow from one idea to the next.

### Voice and Personality
- **Include personal quirks and speech patterns.** If the person uses certain phrases or expressions, weave them in naturally.
- **Show genuine emotion.** Frustration, excitement, uncertainty, humor - humans have feelings about their work.
- **Admit when you don't know something.** Or when something was harder than expected.
- **Use contractions and casual language.** "I'd" instead of "I would", "can't" instead of "cannot".

### Authentic Details
- **Include imperfect moments.** The meeting that ran long, the bug that took three days to find, the time you were completely wrong about something.
- **Mention specific names, dates, technologies.** But don't make it sound like a Wikipedia entry.
- **Reference actual conversations.** "Sarah from the design team pointed out..." or "I remember thinking..."
- **Include sensory details.** "After staring at code for 12 hours straight..." or "During our 6 AM crisis call..."

### Natural Flow
- **Write how you'd actually explain it to a colleague.** Not how you'd present it at a conference.
- **Use transitional thoughts.** "Speaking of which..." or "That reminds me..." or "But here's the thing..."
- **Circle back to earlier points.** Humans don't think linearly - they connect dots throughout.
- **End with genuine reflection.** Not a perfect bow-tied conclusion.

## Specific Techniques

### Opening Paragraphs
❌ **AI Style:** "In today's rapidly evolving tech landscape, system migration has become a critical challenge for organizations..."

✅ **Human Style:** "I still remember the exact moment I realized we were screwed. It was 3 PM on a Tuesday, and our entire API layer had just become obsolete overnight. Not deprecated - obsolete. Facebook's announcement felt like a punch to the gut."

### Story Integration
❌ **AI Style:** "A successful migration requires careful planning and execution."

✅ **Human Style:** "Look, I've been through enough migrations to know that no matter how much you plan, something will go sideways. The trick isn't avoiding problems - it's building enough flexibility to handle them when they show up."

### Technical Explanations
❌ **AI Style:** "Event Sourcing provides comprehensive audit trails and state reconstruction capabilities."

✅ **Human Style:** "Event Sourcing is like having a security camera for your data. Instead of just knowing what your data looks like now, you can replay everything that happened to get there. Super useful when your CFO asks 'How did this customer's balance get so messed up?' and you actually have an answer."

### Conclusions
❌ **AI Style:** "In conclusion, these strategies will help organizations achieve successful digital transformation outcomes."

✅ **Human Style:** "Will all this work for your team? Honestly, I don't know. Every situation is different. But these approaches have saved my bacon more times than I can count, so they're worth considering."

- **Brazilian warmth.** Friendly, welcoming, but still professional.
- **Problem-solver mindset.** Focus on practical solutions over theoretical frameworks.
- **Mentorship voice.** Teaching without talking down to people.

## Company vs Personal Branding Guidelines

### The Toki Labs Context
- **Toki Labs has always existed** - Saulo is transitioning from promoting his personal brand to promoting the company brand
- **It was never a solo operation** - All projects involved teams, even when Saulo was the face of the work
- **Clear timeline distinction** - Separate work done at other companies (Crossover, Rocket Money) from Toki Labs work

### Voice and Attribution Rules
- **Use "we" for current Toki Labs work** - "We built this system", "Our approach to...", "At Toki Labs, we..."
- **Use "I" for personal experience/perspective** - "I learned that...", "In my experience...", "I remember when..."
- **Clear context for external work** - "During my time at [Company], we faced..." or "While leading a team at [Company]..."
- **Team achievement emphasis** - Highlight collaborative effort over individual brilliance
- **Company capability focus** - Position achievements as examples of Toki Labs' capabilities

### Examples of Good vs Poor Branding

❌ **Poor (sounds like solo consultant):**
"I built a system that reduced processing time by 50%. My approach involved..."

✅ **Good (positions as company capability):**
"We built a system that reduced processing time by 50%. Our approach involved..."

❌ **Poor (unclear attribution):**
"I worked with a client who needed to migrate their legacy system..."

✅ **Good (clear context):**
"During my time at Crossover, we worked with a client who needed to migrate their legacy system. These lessons now inform how we approach migrations at Toki Labs..."

❌ **Poor (individual hero narrative):**
"I solved this crisis by implementing a new architecture that saved the company."

✅ **Good (team leadership narrative):**
"Leading a team of 40 engineers, we navigated this crisis by implementing a new architecture. The collaborative approach we developed now shapes how we handle complex projects at Toki Labs."

## Content Structure Alternatives

Instead of always using:
1. Introduction
2. Key Points (with bullets)
3. Benefits
4. Conclusion

Try:
- **Story → Lesson → Application**
- **Problem → Journey → Solution (with setbacks)**
- **Observation → Deep Dive → Reflection**
- **Question → Exploration → More Questions**

## Red Flags to Avoid

### Words/Phrases That Scream AI:
- "In today's rapidly evolving [industry]..."
- "Let's dive deep into..."
- "Key takeaways include..."
- "Cutting-edge solutions"
- "Revolutionary approach"
- "Game-changing innovation"
- Perfect parallel structure in lists

### Structural Red Flags:
- Every paragraph is the same length
- Every section has exactly 3-4 bullet points
- Headers follow the exact same pattern
- Conclusion that perfectly summarizes everything

## Testing Your Content

### The "Would I Actually Say This?" Test
Read it out loud. Does it sound like something you'd actually say to a colleague over coffee?

### The "Personality Check"
Can you identify any unique voice or personality in the writing? Or could this have been written by anyone?

### The "Specificity Test"
Are there enough specific details that someone who knows this domain would think "Yeah, that's exactly what it's like"?

### The "Human Moments Test"
Are there any moments of uncertainty, humor, frustration, or genuine emotion? Or is it all perfectly polished?

## Sample Prompts for Different Content Types

### Blog Post About Technical Challenge
"Write about [technical topic] from the perspective of someone who has actually dealt with this problem multiple times. Include a specific example from your experience, mention at least one thing that didn't go as planned, and write it like you're explaining it to a colleague who's about to face the same challenge."

### Company About Page
"Write this like you're introducing your company to someone at a conference. Be proud of what you've accomplished but don't oversell it. Include why you started the company and what you've learned along the way. Make it sound like a real conversation, not a marketing brochure."

### Technical Tutorial
"Explain this like you're showing someone how to do it over their shoulder. Include the little details that matter but aren't in the documentation. Mention common mistakes you've made or seen others make. Write it like you actually want them to succeed."

Remember: Humans are imperfect, emotional, and tell stories. AI is polished, logical, and lists features. Choose to be human.
